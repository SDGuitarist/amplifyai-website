The people in front of the camera are protected. SAG-AFTRA covers digital likenesses. The WGA restricts AI writing credit. But the people behind the camera, the writers, composers, editors, and VFX artists, have far fewer protections. The rules are being written right now, and most filmmakers have never read the contracts that affect their work. This workshop gives you the frameworks to navigate that gap.

Who This Is For

Filmmakers who know AI is showing up in their work and do not have a clear framework for deciding what is safe, what is ethical, and what protects their rights. You are not anti-AI. You are uncertain. You want someone who has done the reading.

Writers & Screenwriters

The WGA says AI cannot be a writer. But the rules on AI-assisted writing leave a gap you need to understand. Training data protections are new and untested.

You leave with: a clear map of what the WGA agreement covers, where it stops, and how to protect your work in the gap.

Directors & Producers

You make the decisions about whether AI shows up on your production. You carry the liability if consent or disclosure is mishandled. Festival rules are often stricter than union rules.

You leave with: frameworks for making those calls with your eyes open, from pre-production through submission.

Composers & Music Supervisors

The actors are protected. The writers have some coverage. Composers have almost none. No union training data restrictions. AI music generation is permitted in most festival categories where AI performance is banned.

You leave with: the full picture of where you stand, what protections exist, and what you need to advocate for.

Post-Production & VFX

Your work is invisible to the audience. That makes it easier to replace quietly. Behind-the-camera AI is lower risk for the production. It is not lower risk for you.

You leave with: the tools to articulate why your work matters and how to protect it as AI capability grows.

Also relevant for: documentary filmmakers, film students, festival organizers, and anyone making decisions about AI use in creative production. No technical background needed.

The Behind the Camera / In Front of It Analysis

SAG-AFTRA protects performers from AI replicas. Writers have training data restrictions. Composers have almost nothing. This asymmetry is the central argument of the manifesto. At the workshop, you map exactly where your role falls and what protections apply to your work.

The Three Questions in Practice

A director considering AI for pre-visualization. A composer using AI to generate temp tracks. A screenwriter brainstorming with AI assistance. Each scenario gets the same three questions applied, and each produces a different answer. You practice on your own projects, live, with feedback.

Common Questions

Is this anti-AI?

No. Alex uses AI every day in his business. This workshop is about knowing where the lines are, not about avoiding AI. The frameworks help you use AI with your eyes open.

Do I need to understand AI to attend?

No. You need to understand your own work. The workshop assumes familiarity with AI tools but not with the contracts, case law, or ethical frameworks that govern their use in film. That is what we cover.

I am not in a union. Is this still relevant?

Yes. Union contracts set the precedents, but the frameworks apply whether you are union or non-union. Festival policies, copyright implications, and documentation practices affect every filmmaker.

What if I can't make it after I pay?

Full refund up to 48 hours before the workshop. After that, your spot transfers to a future session.

What do I need to bring?

A laptop with a web browser. That is it. We will set up everything else during the session.

Is this legal advice?

No. Alex is a practitioner, not a lawyer. The frameworks are built from verified research into union contracts, festival policies, and case law. They help you ask better questions and make informed decisions. They are not a substitute for legal counsel on your specific project.
