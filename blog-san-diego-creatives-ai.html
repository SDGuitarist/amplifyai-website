A San Diego Creative's Guide to Using AI Well

San Diego has a real creative economy — a film festival scene, working musicians in every neighborhood, media producers, small studios, teachers. And right now most of us are figuring out AI in real time, with no map, between deadlines.

There's plenty of AI training in town, but almost all of it is built for someone else. Chamber workshops on scaling your small business. University sessions on AI in the enterprise. Bootcamps for engineers. Useful if you run a logistics company. Not much help if your job is to make something people feel.

So this is the guide I wish existed when I started: how a working creative in San Diego can actually use AI well, without the hype and without giving away the part that makes the work yours.

Start with what stays human

Before you touch a tool, get clear on what you're not handing over. Your taste. Your voice. The read of a room that took you years. The relationships you've built at every venue and set in this city. That list is your creative moat, and no model has it.

Everything else — the admin, the follow-ups, the turning-one-thing-into-many — is fair game. The whole skill is keeping that line clear so AI amplifies you instead of impersonating you. If you want a fast read on where you're starting from, the two-minute AI check will place you and name your next step.

Point it at the drag, not the craft

The highest-value place for AI in a creative business is almost never the creative part. It's the pile of work around the work: the inquiry that sat unanswered because you were on stage, the invoice you keep forgetting, the recap posts you never make. That's where the hours leak, and that's where a little AI buys back your best time.

The free frameworks on this site are built for exactly that — a way to give AI enough context to be useful, decide what's safe to delegate, and catch the assumptions it quietly makes.

Protect your rights while you're at it

Creative work carries a rights question most business AI training skips entirely: consent, credit, and compensation. If you're a filmmaker or musician, using AI well means using it without stepping on other creatives — or on your future self. That judgment call is worth building before you need it, not after.

Learn it in a room, with your own examples

You can read your way to the ideas, but the thing that actually sticks is doing it with your own work in front of you. That's what the workshops are for — a direct conversation for filmmakers, musicians, and creative teams, built in the room instead of behind a slide deck. Everyone leaves having used something on a real project, not with a pile of theory.

I've run these at the San Diego Streaming Film Festival, at FilmCon, and with local media teams — because the examples land harder when they come from the same scene you work in.

The San Diego version of the whole thing

You don't need to become a prompt engineer. You need to keep doing the work only you can do, and let AI carry the rest — on your terms, in your voice, with your name on what ships. That's true anywhere. It's just a little easier to figure out with someone who works in the same city, at the same venues, on the same kind of deadlines.
