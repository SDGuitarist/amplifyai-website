AI, Consent and Credit: A Working Creative's Field Guide

The conversation about AI and creative rights is mostly happening in rooms you're not in. Guild negotiations. Law firm blog posts. Panels at Cannes. It's important work, and it's also written for people with legal teams, not for the filmmaker cutting a trailer tonight or the musician deciding whether to use an AI voice on a demo.

So here's the working-creative version. Same principles the guilds are fighting over — consent, credit, compensation — but pointed at the decision you actually have to make: how do I use these tools without becoming the thing I'd be furious about?

Because that's the part people miss. Most coverage treats creatives as the victims of AI — your work scraped, your voice cloned. True. But the moment you open an AI tool for your own project, you're on the other side of the table. Now you're the one who could be cutting corners on someone else's consent, credit, or pay. The three C's aren't just protection from AI. They're a standard for how you use it.

Consent: whose voice, face, or style is feeding this?

Before you generate, ask what the tool is drawing on. Cloning a specific singer's tone, a specific actor's likeness, a living artist's unmistakable style — that's using someone without asking. "The tool let me" isn't consent. Nobody signed off just because a model made it easy.

The practical test: if the person whose voice, face, or signature style you're leaning on saw the final piece, would they feel borrowed-from or ripped off? Generic and general is usually fine. Specific and identifiable is where you need a yes, not a shrug.

Credit: are you being honest about what the machine did?

This one is about you, not the audience's lawyers. You don't owe the world a footnote on every AI-assisted comma. But when AI did something a person would normally be credited for — wrote the score, generated the key visual, performed the voice — quietly passing it off as fully hand-made is a debt that comes due. Audiences are getting good at spotting it, and the trust you lose when they catch you is worth more than the credit you saved.

Honest doesn't mean apologetic. "Built with AI assistance, directed by me" is a perfectly proud sentence. The goal is that nobody who trusts your work ever feels lied to about how it was made.

Compensation: who did this used to pay?

When AI takes over a piece of work, notice who used to get that check. Sometimes it's nobody — it's your own admin time, and good, take it back. But sometimes it's the session player, the illustrator, the editor you'd have hired. Replacing a collaborator with a model isn't automatically wrong, but doing it without thinking is how an industry hollows itself out one "efficiency" at a time.

You don't have to keep every job alive out of guilt. You do have to make the call with your eyes open, and decide which relationships are worth more than the money AI saves you.

The rule underneath all three

If you want one line to carry into every project: don't do to other creatives with AI what you'd be furious about if it were done to you.

That's not a legal standard. It's a judgment standard, and judgment is exactly the thing that doesn't come in the tool. The people who'll come out of this era with their reputations intact aren't the ones who used AI the least. They're the ones who used it without quietly selling out the people around them — including their future selves.

Your tools, your voice, your rights

This is the heart of the workshop I run for filmmakers and musicians. Not "AI bad" or "AI good," but the practical judgment for using powerful tools while keeping your voice, your authorship, and your relationships intact. The tools aren't going away. The question is whether you use them like someone who intends to still be respected in five years.
